Redwoods are survivors of abuse who grow above and beyond, and it’s not an uncommon story. The Redwood seed needs a fire to be freed from its shell—then they become the tallest trees in the world.

Jen

Jen is a Redwood. She sold her consulting company two years ago to run one of the most effective environmental organizations in the west. She’s also a sought-after social media expert.

We are all seeds of potential, but the redwood seed needs a forest fire to free it from its shell. This living hell can result in remarkable growth.

Jen

Jen’s hell was an abusive alcoholic who lived off her income. When she tried to stand up to him, he punched her in the head so hard she didn’t remember. If she left, she knew he would put her on the list of people he planned to kill before he killed himself. She finally walked away after he pointed his gun at refugee kids. She’ll never know why he didn’t shoot her then.

When all seems lost, new life emerges.

Jen

As Jen sat amid the wreckage of her life, an acquaintance insisted they get together. Laura, the capable head of a investment company told Jen the story of her own hell. Jen was dumbfound. How could the same thing happen to Laura, who was strong and confident? Laura explained that it was not unusual for accomplished people to have an abused history. She told Jen about other people with similar experiences and even more impressive accomplishments.

You are not alone and your future is bright.

Jen

Laura was the first of many people to tell Jen her story of survival and triumph. Jen’s image of an abuse survivor, beaten down and broken, was replaced with these high achievers, these Redwoods. They were all around her embodying the message, “You are not alone and your future is bright.”

How I Got Out I stashed clothes, important documents and money at a friends months before, but I couldn’t physically make my limbs move to leave. One day, he threatened someone else convincingly enough that something inside me snapped. I methodically gathered my computer, dog and purse while he followed me through the house asking what I was doing. I said I was going for a drive and walked out. I couldn’t believe he wasn’t following me. Then he was. Walking down a long driveway to the garage, I was sure he would shoot me in the back. I’m still surprised I got away. I wish I had done it sooner, preferably when he wasn’t there, but I got out.
